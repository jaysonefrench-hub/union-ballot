# Compliance Mapping — Union Ballot

This document maps each requirement of (1) the LMRDA as interpreted by the DOL Office of Labor-Management Standards in its Compliance Tip *Electing Union Officers Using Remote Electronic Voting Systems* (updated December 10, 2024), and (2) the IAFF Legal Department's *Best Practices and Model Rules* to the specific feature of this system that satisfies it. It also lists the obligations that remain procedural — things the election committee must do that no software can do for them. This document, together with the README and source code, is suitable as the core of the procedures packet a Local submits to the IAFF Legal Department when seeking approval to use electronic voting for secret-ballot matters.

An important scoping note: most IAFF locals are composed entirely of public-sector employees and are therefore not directly covered by the LMRDA's Title IV election provisions (coverage attaches when a local has or seeks even one private-sector member). This system is built to the LMRDA standard regardless, because it is the strictest applicable benchmark, many states apply comparable requirements to public-sector union elections, and the IAFF model rules incorporate the same principles.

## Requirement-by-requirement mapping

| Requirement | Source | How this system satisfies it |
|---|---|---|
| Secret ballot: vote "cast in such a manner that the person expressing such choice cannot be identified with the choice expressed" | 29 U.S.C. 402(k), 481(a)(b); IAFF model rule (3) | Ballots are sealed-box encrypted at cast time and stored with only a random UUID, election number, and ciphertext — no member ID, credential reference, or timestamp, in a table with no physical insertion order. No join path exists between voter and ballot anywhere in the schema. |
| Avoid a persistent voter–vote link; credentials randomly assigned after eligibility determined; no trail between credential and voted ballot | OLMS Tip §1 & fn.4 | Eligibility is frozen from the roster *before* credentials exist; credentials are CSPRNG-generated and stored only as salted hashes; the ballot record never references the credential. The encrypted member↔credential map (used solely to void-and-reissue lost credentials, which OLMS expects) cannot reach any ballot. |
| Randomize stored vote order so tally reveals nothing about casting order | OLMS Tip §1 | Ballots are keyed by random UUID in a `WITHOUT ROWID` table (storage order = UUID order, not arrival order) and are additionally shuffled before decryption at the tally. Credential redemption is recorded by date only. |
| Encrypted ballot file openable only through simultaneous use of multiple decryption keys held by a diverse group (competing candidates' reps + neutrals) | OLMS Tip §1 | The election private key is split via Shamir's Secret Sharing (configurable, default 3-of-5) at creation, displayed exactly once, and never stored. The tally requires the threshold number of shares entered together; the reconstructed key lives only in memory for the count. |
| Secure portal / restrict credential exposure | OLMS Tip §1; IAFF model rule (4) password protection | Voting requires only the high-entropy (~80-bit) one-time credential over HTTPS; committee and observer functions are password-protected accounts (bcrypt). Credentials are never displayed after issuance. `Cache-Control: no-store` on every page. |
| Detect and log attempts to breach the ballot file / tamper alerts to observers | OLMS Tip §§1–2 | Every rejected credential, failed sign-in, failed key reconstruction, failed decryption, and ballot-count/credential-count mismatch is written to a hash-chained audit log; observers see live chain verification and integrity alerts on their dashboard. |
| Prevent ballot image capture and sharing | OLMS Tip §1 & fn.5 | The confirmation screen never echoes the voter's choices, removing the primary proof artifact for vote buying or coercion. (No browser can technically prevent screenshots; OLMS notes capture capability alone is not a violation.) |
| Observer rights: observe credential preparation/distribution (without seeing individual credentials), verify the system on practice ballots, observe the count, inspect source code or rely on an audit | 29 U.S.C. 481(c); OLMS Tip §2 | Read-only observer accounts (one per candidate) see configuration, keyholder lists, issuance counts, turnout, results, and the full audit log. Test elections let observers cast practice ballots and watch a full tally. The system is source-available for direct inspection — the OLMS-favored "open source code" path — and the tally ceremony is designed to be conducted with observers physically or virtually present. |
| Observers may compile a list of who has voted | 29 C.F.R. 452 tradition; OLMS Tip §2 | The turnout list (name, date, method — never choices) is visible to observers and the committee. |
| Detailed transaction logs with integrity assurance | OLMS Tip §2 | Append-only audit log where each entry commits to the hash of its predecessor; any alteration breaks the chain, and verification runs on every observer page load. Ballot casting is logged only as anonymous counter events. |
| One person, one vote / adequate safeguards | 29 U.S.C. 481(c); IAFF model rule (2) | Credentials are single-use, enforced atomically in a transaction (a race between two submissions of the same credential cannot yield two ballots). Reissue voids the old credential and is blocked entirely once a credential has been redeemed. At tally, sealed-ballot count is checked against redeemed-credential count and any mismatch raises a logged integrity alert. |
| Records preserved one year: eligibility lists, credentials, log files, timestamped code, encrypted ballot files, tally results | 29 U.S.C. 481(e); OLMS Tip §3 | One-click records archive exports the election configuration, eligibility snapshot, turnout, hashed credentials, encrypted ballots, results, and the complete verified audit log. Ballots remain stored encrypted after the tally. |
| Right to vote / alternative method for members without internet access or who choose not to vote electronically; no accessibility barriers | OLMS Tip §4; IAFF model rule (alternative way to vote) | Members without email or flagged for paper are excluded from electronic credentials and listed for the paper alternative; paper-ballot receipt is recorded so the member is marked voted. The UI is server-rendered semantic HTML that works without JavaScript for casting, with visible focus states and reduced-motion support. |
| Sufficient encryption for security and authenticity; members-only access; ability to calculate total votes | IAFF model rules (1), (5) | X25519/XSalsa20-Poly1305 sealed boxes (NaCl), AES-256-GCM for the reissue map, SHA-256 hash chain; access gated by member credentials; automated tally with per-race totals, majority/two-thirds/plurality thresholds, and IAFF runoff detection. |
| IAFF majority-and-runoff rule for officer elections | IAFF Sample Local CBL | The tally declares a winner only on a majority of ballots cast in the race and otherwise flags a required runoff between the top two candidates. Two-thirds thresholds are supported for bylaw amendments. |
| 15-day written notice of nominations and election | IAFF Sample Local CBL; 29 U.S.C. 481(e) | Notice-sent date is recorded on each election and shown to observers (procedural obligation — see below). |
| IAFF Legal Department approval before electronic voting on secret-ballot matters (officer elections, delegate elections, dues rates) | IAFF Best Practices and Model Rules | The system refuses to create a binding officer, delegate, or dues vote unless the committee records the IAFF Legal Department approval reference, which is preserved in the election record and audit log. Test elections are exempt so the platform can be demonstrated during the approval process. |

## Procedural obligations the software cannot satisfy

Software can enforce architecture; it cannot attend meetings. The election committee remains responsible for: obtaining the IAFF Legal Department's actual approval of this platform and your procedures before any binding secret-ballot vote (the app records the approval; it cannot grant it); mailing the 15-day written notice of nominations and election to each member's last known address; conducting nominations per your CBL; determining good standing correctly before issuing credentials; actually inviting each candidate's observer to credential issuance, the test run, and the tally ceremony, and conducting the tally with keyholders and observers present; providing the paper-ballot alternative and hand-counting those ballots with observers, then adding them to the electronic totals before certifying; publishing results to the membership; retaining the exported records archive, the deployed source code, and the server for one year; and following any runoff requirement your CBL imposes. Deploy only over HTTPS, use an email provider the local controls for credential delivery, and instruct members to delete the credential email after voting.

## Honest limitations

OLMS evaluates every system case-by-case and offers no safe harbor; building to its published guidance is the strongest available position, not a certification. Email delivery necessarily places the credential in the member's inbox in plaintext transit — the same exposure as a mailed credential letter, and the reason the system never stores plaintext itself. Whoever controls the server could alter this software; that is exactly what the observer rights, source inspection, test elections, and the tamper-evident log are designed to surface, and why the deployed code must be preserved with the election records. Finally, this system and documentation do not constitute legal advice; have your local's counsel and the IAFF Legal Department review both before a binding election.
